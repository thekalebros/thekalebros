Handforged Damascus Ring

So, you built yourself a forge, found a heavy chunk of metal for your first anvil, now what? Let's make a ring!

First, we need to find some sort of material to make the ring out of. "Cable Damascus" is a fun material to work with/make. An old timer from Oregon named Wayne Goddard wrote a book on the subject and gave me some inspiration.

I always keep my eyes peeled for old chunks of wire rope laying around. Junk yards sometimes have twisted up lengths for free.

First I section the rope into manageble lengths. Use whatever saw you have available, hacksaws work well. Next I like to unwind the large strands before putting them into a fire to burn off the oil and grease embedded in them to protect against corrosion. You can toss them in a bonfire like i did, or simply put them in your forge.

After you've removed the tar, it's time to re-rind the rope together. I replaced the center strand with a solid piece of steel rod this time. Let's see how it affects the pattern when we're done!

Tack welding the ends together is an excellent time saver if available. If not, I would wind wire around the ends to help hold it together for the first few "heats". Because I had access to a welder, I went ahead and added some scrap metal for a handle.

Finally some action! Start slow as you "mash" the wires together. I use a vise to help "twist" the billet on itself to help keep it solid.

After I have it welded up, I cut the billet into 4 sections, welded them up side by side and back to the forge we go.

Here we have a similar length of wire rope next to our condensed and quartered billet cooling off.

Cross section of the four pieces welded side by side.

Sample section sawed off the end of the billet

Sneak peak of the pattern so far.

Since I like the pattern so far, I cut a thicker section off and drill a hole through the center. Once we have the piece up to temperature, I use a hot punch to progressively enlarge the center of the ring.

What I ended up with.

After a few passes on the grinder, it's time for hand tools. If you have a lathe, even better!

Once I was satisfied with the finish (think loooong time with sandpaper) I heated it up with a hand torch before quencing in oil. Not toally necessary, but this way I won't have to worry about dings and scratches forming in the steel as easily.

The finished product!

While the pattern is subtle, I enjoyed learning how to work with what I had on hand to overcome a new set of obstacles.

Dirt Cheap Forge

Metal working/forging is a very rewarding skill. With simple hand tools anyone (given enough time) can create their very own handcrafted blades and more! One crucial tool is a forge. Today we will be constructing a simple brick forge capable of handling most blade forging/finishing work.

Quick and dirty (HOT!) forge/burner example I threw together recently. For a first time metal worker, I typically recommend you start with a regular Propane burner assembly. However, once you've outgrown these options, here's how one might "level up" for cheap.

Our componants for the project, you can find these items at any hardware store.

After drilling two holes in the iron piping to fit the brass through i mock it up to make sure it all fits correctly.

Next we drill a small "jet" hole into the brass tube.

Final assembly gets some extra strength teflon tape.

Not pictured is when I used JB Weld to fix the brass "jet" tube in position. The idea is to direct it down the center of the iron burner tube. Makes sense? Good.

Now for the easy part! I bought a dozen or so fire "refractory" bricks from the local wood stove shop. (they do exist!) I simply tacked a few braces to keep the bricks in place. You could even get away with simply stacking them in place, which I have done on multiple occasions.

At this point you should be a propane source away from a working forge. It may be a good idea to test for leaks in the system. Some soap water in a spray bottle does great. Just spray along the pressurized line and watch for bubbles to form.

Will it win any beauty contests? Probably not. But will it heat up steel to a workable temperature?

I'd say it works!
